Chemistry And Biology Behind Addiction

The Chemistry

Neurotransmitters: The brain’s neurotransmitters play a role in device use. Dopamine is central to the pleasure and reward associated with using devices.
Device Interaction with Neurotransmitters:

Devices (e.g., smartphones, gaming consoles) can mimic natural neurotransmitters by providing instant gratification (e.g., notifications, likes).

Overuse of devices disrupts normal communication between neurons, affecting brain function.

Brain Areas Affected:

Basal Ganglia: Responsible for habit formation. Frequent device use over-activates this circuit, reinforcing the behaviour.

Prefrontal Cortex: Involved in decision-making and impulse control.Excessive device use impairs these functions.

Amygdala: Influences emotional responses related to device content.

Hippocampus: Important for memory. Device use affects memory formation and recall.

The Biology

Brain Circuitry Changes:

Device dependence alters brain circuits, making it harder to regulate usage.

Repetitive device use strengthens these pathways, reinforcing the behaviour.

Reward Pathways and Habit Formation:

Brain’s reward pathways encourage repeated device interactions.

Devices hijack this system, leading to dependence.

Memory and Emotional Impact:

Device use affects memory and emotional responses (e.g., social media-induced emotions).

Definition Of ‘Digital Addiction’

Digital addiction refers to a harmful dependence on digital media and high-tech devices. Although it is not a formally diagnosable disorder, some psychologists believe that addiction to digital devices and media is similar to substance addictions.

This occurs when technology harms the user’s life and is difficult to stop despite its harmful effects. It encompasses compulsive use of digital devices such as smartphones, video games, computers, social media, and the internet.

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) includes a description of internet gaming disorder, which is a specific form of digital addiction related to online gaming. Although not officially recognized as a diagnosis, internet gaming disorder is characterized by negative impacts on the gamer’s life, causing impairment or distress.

 Symptoms may include:

  • intense preoccupation with gaming.

  • withdrawal symptoms when unable to play.

  • increasing amounts of gaming to satisfy the urge.

  • unsuccessful attempts to stop playing.

  • loss of interest in other activities.

  • using gaming to cope with emotions.

    • How Does It Occur?

    Brain Development and Digital Media:
    Adolescent Brain:

    • During adolescence, the brain undergoes significant changes. The prefrontal cortex, responsible for decision-making and impulse control, is still developing. At the same time, the reward system (involving dopamine) is highly active.

    Reward Circuitry:

    • Digital media, such as social media and video games, can activate the brain’s reward system.

    • When teens engage with screens, they receive pleasurable feelings due to dopamine release.

    Incomplete Maturation:

    • Adolescents’ brains are not fully matured, which affects their ability to regulate impulses and make informed decisions. They may prioritize immediate rewards (like social media likes) over long-term consequences.

    Genetic Predisposition:
    Dopamine Signalling:

    • Genetic variations related to dopamine signalling regulation play a crucial role in addiction risk. It’s not just about the amount of dopamine but how it’s regulated.

    Impulsivity and Reward Circuitry:

    • Some teens have a genetic predisposition toward high impulsivity and an overactive reward circuitry. 

    • This combination makes them more susceptible to addictive behaviours, including digital media use.

    Digital Addiction Mechanism:
    Reinforcement:

    • Teens experience positive reinforcement when using digital devices (e.g., getting likes on social media or achieving game levels). This reinforces the behaviour.

    Escapism:

    • Digital media can serve as an escape from stress or negative emotions. Teens may turn to screens to cope with challenges.

    Compulsive Use:

    • Over time, teens may develop compulsive habits, unable to control their screen time despite negative consequences (e.g., poor grades, strained relationships).

    The Addiction Cycle

    Brain Activation:

    • Teens engage with digital media (e.g., social media, video games).

    • Brain’s reward system activates (dopamine release).

    Positive Reinforcement:

    • Immediate rewards (likes, achievements) reinforce behaviour.

    • Pleasurable feelings encourage continued use.

    Incomplete Brain Maturation:

    • Adolescent brains are still developing.

    • Prefrontal cortex (decision-making) not fully mature.

    • Impulsivity and prioritizing short-term rewards.

    Genetic Predisposition:

    • Genetic variations affect dopamine signalling.

    • Overactive reward circuitry.

    • Some teens more susceptible to addiction.

    Compulsive Habits:

    • Escapism: Screens used to cope with stress.

    • Teens develop compulsive digital habits.

    • Difficulty controlling screen time.

    Negative Consequences:

    • Poor grades, strained relationships.

    • Impact on mental health and well-being.

    Why Cant We Stop?

    Immediate Rewards:

    • Digital media provides instant gratification (likes, achievements, notifications), activating the brain’s reward system. This positive reinforcement encourages continued use.

    Incomplete Brain Maturation:

    • Adolescent brains are still developing, especially the prefrontal cortex responsible for decision-making. Teens prioritize short-term rewards over long-term consequences.

    Genetic Factors: Some individuals have genetic variations related to dopamine signalling. An overactive reward circuitry makes them more susceptible to addictive behaviours.

    Escapism:

    • Screens serve as an escape from stress, boredom, or negative emotions. People turn to digital media to cope, reinforcing the habit.

    Compulsive Habits:

    • Over time, digital use becomes compulsive. Despite negative consequences (poor grades, strained relationships), individuals struggle to control their screen time.

    How to combat the addiction

    Awareness and Self-Assessment:

    • Understand your digital habits. Reflect on how much time you spend online and whether it affects your well-being.

    Set Boundaries:

    • Establish clear rules for screen time. Use features like app limits or screen time tracking.

    Digital Detox:

    • Take regular breaks from screens. Unplug for a day or weekend.

    Mindful Usage:

    • Be intentional. Ask yourself: “Why am I using this app or device?”

    Replace with Offline Activities:

    • Engage in hobbies, exercise, or face-to-face interactions.

    Seek Social Support:

    • Connect with friends and family. Share your goals to reduce screen time.

    Professional Help:

    • If addiction persists, consult a therapist or counsellor.
